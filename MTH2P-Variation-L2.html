Lesson 2: Direct and Partial Variation

If you get paid by the hour, does your pay start at zero? What if you're a plumber who charges a service fee just to show up?

Core Concepts: Does it Start at Zero?

Today we're looking at two types of linear relations. They both create straight lines, but they start at different places.

1. Direct Variation

In direct variation, the relationship starts at the origin (0, 0). If you do zero work, you get zero pay. The initial value is always zero.

  • The y-intercept ($b$) is always 0.
  • The equation is simpler: $y = mx$.
  • The graph is a straight line that passes **directly through the origin**.

Example: Earning $20 per hour. The graph starts at (0, 0).

2. Partial Variation

In partial variation, the relationship starts with an initial value that is **not** zero. There is a fixed, flat fee before the rate of change begins.

  • The y-intercept ($b$) is **not** 0.
  • The equation is $y = mx + b$.
  • The graph is a straight line that **does not** pass through the origin.

Example: A $50 flat fee, plus $20 per hour. The graph starts at (0, 50).

Guided Practice: Banquet Hall Rental

A banquet hall charges a $200 rental fee, plus $30 per guest for food. Let's analyze this relationship.

1. Does this situation represent direct or partial variation? How do you know?

2. What is the initial value (the flat fee)?

3. What is the rate of change (cost per guest)?

4. Write the equation to model the total cost.

Solution Walkthrough

1. This is partial variation because there is an initial rental fee of $200, even for zero guests.

2. The initial value is the rental fee: **$b = 200**.

3. The rate of change is the cost per guest: **$m = 30**.

4. The equation is **$C = 30g + 200$**.

Recap

  • Direct Variation starts at (0, 0). Its equation is $y = mx$.
  • Partial Variation starts at an initial value, $b$. Its equation is $y = mx + b$.
