Lesson 3: Exponent Laws

Core Concepts: The Rules of Exponents

Exponents are a shorthand way of writing repeated multiplication. The exponent laws are rules that help us simplify expressions involving powers.

1. Multiplication Law

When multiplying powers with the **same base**, you **add** the exponents.

$$ (x^a)(x^b) = x^{a+b} $$

Example: $(x^4)(x^2) = x^{4+2} = x^6$

2. Division Law

When dividing powers with the **same base**, you **subtract** the exponents.

$$ \frac{x^a}{x^b} = x^{a-b} $$

Example: $\frac{x^7}{x^3} = x^{7-3} = x^4$

3. Power of a Power Law

When raising a power to another exponent, you **multiply** the exponents.

$$ (x^a)^b = x^{ab} $$

Example: $(x^5)^3 = x^{5 \times 3} = x^{15}$

4. Zero Exponent Law

Anything (except zero) raised to the power of zero is **1**.

$$ x^0 = 1 $$

Example: $27^0 = 1$

5. Negative Exponent Law

A negative exponent means to take the **reciprocal** of the base.

$$ x^{-a} = \frac{1}{x^a} $$

Example: $x^{-3} = \frac{1}{x^3}$

Independent Practice

Use the exponent laws to simplify each expression.

1. Simplify: $(a^2)(a^6)$

$a^{2+6} = a^8$

2. Simplify: $\frac{b^{10}}{b^5}$

$b^{10-5} = b^5$

3. Simplify: $(c^4)^5$

$c^{4 \times 5} = c^{20}$

4. Simplify: $(3x^2y^5)^3$

$3^3 (x^2)^3 (y^5)^3 = 27x^6y^{15}$
